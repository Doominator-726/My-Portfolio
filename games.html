About

My deep interest in computer science originally began with video game development. At first, I was curious about design as I wished to build my own games. However, I now find myself captivated by the technical aspects of computer science. An appreciation for details and process has enabled me to create virtual worlds with various systems that connect. This has fostered a passion for the field as an important outlet for creativity. Specifically, I have built eight games in the last 3 years using two design engines, Godot and Pygame. Below are brief descriptions of the games.

Cultus Astrorum is a first person shooter demo I created for an IB CAS project. Like every game I have built, this one taught me a great deal about game development. Cultus Astrorum features pathfinding enemies with skeletal animations, multiple weapons, numerous cutscenes, and several player objectives. The game also features a system that allows the player to save progress in the game so that they can pick up where they left off the next time they play. I also focused on environmental interaction - players can break crates, pick up weapons, eat food, flush toilets, and toggle sinks. Other features included 3D models and baked lighting for performance. Overall, the creation of Cultus Astotrum was a fun and challenging project that allowed me to improve my ability to develop 3D games.

Abocastasis Abaddon is a combat focused racing game. Specifically, players compete in a race with A.I. opponents, passing through checkpoints and fighting with other cars while zooming around a track. Players can access five different weapons including miniguns, rockets, and mines. Players can also collect items to repair damage to their cars. This is critical as damage reduces the performance of the car. The game also features threatening physical objects, such as explosive barrels. The checkpoint system is used to track the position of competing cars and ultimately to determine final standings. Abocastasis Abaddon marked my first deep into 3D development and I am satisfied with both the end product and the lessons acquired during its creation.

Warrior in the Garden is a “pseudo-3D” action adventure I created with the Pygame engine. In the game, the player navigates three sets of randomly generated dungeons presenting various challenges and surprises. After passing these tests, the player faces a final confrontation with an alien boss. Along the way, the player must find and use four distinct weapons to overcome ten vicious enemies. The most notable aspect of the game is the 3D effect. With the help of an online tutorial on the Coder Space YouTube channel, I was able to use raycasting and other techniques to give the game a 3D appearance in a technically 2D format. For the randomly generated dungeons, I coded and employed a random walk algorithm inspired by a Unity tutorial. Additionally, I gave the player’s opponents pathfinding capabilities, again using techniques learned from a Coder Space tutorial. Finally, I made use of color theory to enhance the overall look of the game.

ME is an exploration platformer game. In it you go through a level filled with traps and enemies. You must collect four different upgrades as each is necessary to progress. The upgrades range from making certain platforms and enemies visible, to giving the player the power to swap gravity. At the end you fight a Boss - which I based on myself. The most notable thing about this game is the unsettling nature of the game’s graphics. The game features animated enemies, traps and projectiles. These entities use graphics I made using pictures of myself. The game looks weird yet the style is cohesive and unique. I made this game to familiarize myself with the Godot engine. In this respect, the project was a success since I feel I improved my capabilities with the engine over the course of this game’s development.

Minigolf Mayhem is a minigolf game demo with complex level design. It generates maps using .csv files created in a tile based map editor. You navigate these maps using a golf ball that you can swing around. However, you only have a limited number of swings and lives. You must also avoid obstacles such as holes, fire, and water. There are various contraptions in the maps such as arrows which control the direction of the ball, electric lamps that turn on and off, etc. The ball behaves differently on different surfaces. It receives the most friction on sand and the least on ice. The maps are also fitted with various decorations which fade when close to the ball.

Alien game is an action arcade game about staying alive via managing various elements. Your health is represented by a red bar, it is constantly depleting. Killing enemies will give you health but reduce your size. To regain size you must collect the power ups by shooting them. There are two types of projectiles. Teleports collect the power ups but will hurt you if used on enemies. Bullets kill the enemies but will destroy the power ups. In terms of theming, the game is presented as if it was made by aliens. It uses alien language with images and music we have beamed into space.

Flightgoria is a top down flight shooter game. In the game you must pass various enemies before getting to a final boss. The enemies include static enemies that you must fly around, enemies that will shoot projectiles at you, and more. Along the way, you can also collect pickups to make your shots more powerful and to regain health. This game was the second I ever made on my own. Compared to my first game (not shown), I progressed a lot in terms of visuals and gameplay.

In this game you play as a teenager in the early 2010s. You ride down a mountain in a cart avoiding hikers and various obstacles. If you hit an obstacle you stop and lose “Virality Points”. This was a game I made for my AP CSP Create Task. It was inspired by the 1971 game The Oregon Trail. The game mixes the original game with mad libs in a text based adventure. The game's structure is split up into days. Each day you can gather supplies before proceeding into a random event. These random events can include combat, trading, decisions, etc. They are preceded by randomly selected introductory events, such as stopping at a sign to choose a direction to go in. The game has a character system where each character has four stats that determine combat and their actions in certain situations. They also each have their own health values. You start out with five characters but you can gain more, or lose some to combat and disease. The game is filled with various locations, character names, and objects randomly chosen from large lists of strings. You may have Batman find various copies of 1984 one night or go into combat with Napoleon. Overall, this was a fun project and I learned a lot about Python and random generation.
